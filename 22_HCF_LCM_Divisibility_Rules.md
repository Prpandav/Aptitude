# HCF, LCM & Divisibility Rules - Fundamentals

## 1. Formulas

### Divisibility Rules

| Number | Rule                                                         |
| ------ | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| 2      | Last digit is even (0, 2, 4, 6, 8)                           |
| 3      | Sum of digits is divisible by 3                              |
| 4      | Last two digits form a number divisible by 4                 |
| 5      | Last digit is 0 or 5                                         |
| 6      | Divisible by both 2 and 3                                    |
| 8      | Last three digits form a number divisible by 8               |
| 9      | Sum of digits is divisible by 9                              |
| 10     | Last digit is 0                                              |
| 11     | Difference between alternating digit sums is divisible by 11 |

---

### HCF (Highest Common Factor)

The greatest number that divides all given numbers exactly.

**Prime Factorization Method**

```text
HCF = Product of common prime factors with smallest powers
```

---

### LCM (Least Common Multiple)

The smallest number divisible by all given numbers.

**Prime Factorization Method**

```text
LCM = Product of all prime factors with highest powers
```

---

### Relationship Between HCF and LCM

For two numbers:

```text
HCF × LCM = Product of the Numbers
```

or

```text
LCM = (Product of Numbers) ÷ HCF
```

---

## 2. Key Concepts

### Factors

Factors divide a number exactly.

Example:

```text
Factors of 12 = 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12
```

---

### Multiples

Multiples are obtained by repeatedly multiplying a number.

Example:

```text
Multiples of 5 = 5, 10, 15, 20, ...
```

---

### Prime Numbers

Numbers having exactly two factors:

```text
2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13, ...
```

---

### Prime Factorization

Breaking a number into prime factors.

Example:

```text
60 = 2² × 3 × 5
```

---

### When to Use HCF?

* Splitting items into largest equal groups
* Finding greatest possible size
* Distribution problems

---

### When to Use LCM?

* Repeating events
* Synchronization problems
* Finding least common quantity

---

## 3. Shortcuts

### Shortcut 1

For two numbers:

```text
LCM = (a × b) ÷ HCF
```

Example:

```text
HCF = 6
Numbers = 18 and 24

LCM = (18 × 24) ÷ 6
     = 72
```

---

### Shortcut 2

If two numbers are co-prime:

```text
HCF = 1
LCM = Product of numbers
```

Example:

```text
8 and 15

LCM = 120
```

---

### Shortcut 3

Quick Divisibility by 9

Keep adding digits until one digit remains.

Example:

```text
567

5 + 6 + 7 = 18
1 + 8 = 9

Therefore divisible by 9.
```

---

### Shortcut 4

Quick Divisibility by 11

Example:

```text
121

(1 + 1) − 2 = 0

Therefore divisible by 11.
```

---

## 4. Common Mistakes

### Confusing Factors and Multiples

```text
Factors of 12 are finite.

Multiples of 12 are infinite.
```

---

### Using Largest Powers for HCF

Use smallest powers for HCF.

Example:

```text
24 = 2³ × 3
36 = 2² × 3²

HCF = 2² × 3 = 12
```

---

### Using Smallest Powers for LCM

Use highest powers for LCM.

---

### Forgetting HCF × LCM Formula

Applicable only for two numbers.

## 5. Solved Examples

### Example 1

Find HCF of 24 and 36.

**Solution**

```text
24 = 2³ × 3
36 = 2² × 3²

HCF = 2² × 3
    = 12
```

---

### Example 2

Find LCM of 12 and 18.

**Solution**

```text
12 = 2² × 3
18 = 2 × 3²

LCM = 2² × 3²
    = 36
```

---

### Example 3

Is 7,128 divisible by 9?

**Solution**

```text
7 + 1 + 2 + 8 = 18

18 is divisible by 9

Therefore 7,128 is divisible by 9.
```

---

### Example 4

The bells ring every 12, 15, and 20 minutes. When will they ring together again?

**Solution**

```text
LCM(12,15,20)

12 = 2² × 3
15 = 3 × 5
20 = 2² × 5

LCM = 60

Answer: 60 minutes
```

# Data Interpretation - Fundamentals

## 1. Formulas

Data Interpretation (DI) introduces no new math. It strictly relies on three foundational tools from Phase 1: Percentages, Ratios, and Averages.

**Percentage Increase / Decrease (Growth Rate):**
$$
\text{\% Change} = \left( \frac{\text{Final} - \text{Initial}}{\text{Initial}} \right) \times 100
$$

**Pie Chart Conversions:**
* **Percentage to Degree:** $\text{Degrees} = \text{Percentage} \times 3.6$
* **Degree to Percentage:** $\text{Percentage} = \frac{\text{Degrees}}{3.6}$

## 2. Key Concepts

- **Visual Filtration:** DI tests your ability to isolate specific data points and ignore the noise. Never calculate the entire table—only calculate exactly what the question demands.
- **Core Chart Types:**
  - **Tables:** Precise, but slow to read.
  - **Bar/Line Graphs:** Best for rapid visual estimation of trends and growth gaps.
  - **Pie Charts:** Represents fractional parts of a whole ($100\%$ or $360^\circ$).
- **Uniform Bases:** When comparing averages or ratios across multiple sectors, always verify they reference the exact same total sample universe ($N$).

## 3. Shortcuts

- **The 10% / 1% Anchor:** Never write out basic percentages. For ₹45,600:
  - **10%** = ₹4,560 (move decimal 1 place left).
  - **1%** = ₹456 (move decimal 2 places left).
  - *Build from here:* 5% is half of 10%. 11% is 10% + 1%.
- **Bracket Estimation:** To evaluate $\frac{412}{893}$ mentally, simplify to $\frac{410}{890} \approx \frac{41}{89}$. Since 41 is just under half of 89 ($44.5$), the answer is slightly under 50%.
- **Denominator Bypassing:** To find the "highest growth year," don't calculate exact fractions. Look for the largest visual gap between the numerator (the change) and the denominator (the base).
- **Last-Digit Checks:** When summing large tables under time pressure, add *only* the unit digits. If only one multiple-choice option ends in that digit, pick it and move on.
- **Scale Invariance:** When calculating ratios, ignore unit scaling (e.g., "in millions") entirely. $45M : 30M$ is exactly the same as $45 : 30$.

## 4. Common Mistakes

- **The Missing Unit Scale:** Ignoring the chart's header multiplier (e.g., reading "45" instead of "45,000" or "45 Lakhs").
- **Base Year Swap:** In "percentage growth from 2022 to 2023", always use the older year (2022) as the denominator.
- **Degree vs. Percentage:** 30% on a pie chart is *not* $30^\circ$. It is $(30 \times 3.6) = 108^\circ$.
- **Absolute vs. Percentage Growth:** A jump from 10 to 20 is a $100\%$ growth. A jump from 100 to 120 is only $20\%$ growth, despite a larger absolute gain. 

## 5. Solved Examples

**Data Source for Examples:** 
*Vehicles produced (in thousands) by Company X over three years.*
| Type | 2023 | 2024 | 2025 |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Sedans** | 35 | 42 | 48 |
| **SUVs**   | 20 | 28 | 32 |
| **EVs**    | 5  | 12 | 25 |

### Easy (Ratio Focus)
**Problem:** What is the ratio of EVs produced in 2023 to the SUVs produced in 2025?
* **Step 1:** Isolate data: EVs in 2023 = 5; SUVs in 2025 = 32.
* **Final Answer:** $\mathbf{5 : 32}$.

### Medium (Average Focus)
**Problem:** Calculate the average number of Sedans produced per year across the given three-year period.
* **Step 1:** Sum the row items: $35 + 42 + 48 = 125$.
* **Step 2:** Divide by the 3 periods.
* **Final Answer:** $\frac{125}{3} = \mathbf{41.67 \text{ thousand}}$.

### Placement-Level (Growth Focus)
**Problem:** Which vehicle type registered the highest percentage growth in production from 2023 to 2025?
* **Sedans:** Change $= 13 \implies \frac{13}{35} \approx 37\%$
* **SUVs:** Change $= 12 \implies \frac{12}{20} = 60\%$
* **EVs:** Change $= 20 \implies \frac{20}{5} = 400\%$
* **Final Answer:** **EVs** ($400\%$).

**Case 2: Multi-Sector Pie Chart (Questions 6-10)**
*Distribution of total students ($N = 1800$) across 5 engineering branches:*
**CS:** 30% | **IT:** 25% | **ECE:** 20% | **ME:** 15% | **CE:** 10%

### Beginner Questions (10)
1. What is the average amount of interest on loans paid by the company during this entire 5-year period?
2. The total amount of bonus paid by the company in 2022 is approximately what percent of the total salary paid in that same year?
3. Total expenditure on Fuel and Transport in 2021 is what percentage of the Fuel and Transport expenditure in 2025?
4. What is the ratio of the total expenditure on Taxes across all years to the total expenditure on Salary across all years?
5. In which year was the total expenditure across all five categories combined the lowest?
6. How many total students are enrolled in the CS and IT departments combined?
7. What is the central angle (in degrees) corresponding to the Electronics (ECE) sector?
8. If 20% of the students from the Mechanical branch transfer to the CS branch, what will be the new total number of students in CS?
9. What is the ratio of students studying Civil to those studying Information Technology?
10. The number of students in the ECE branch is what percentage more than the number of students in Civil?

### Intermediate Questions (5)
11. If the IT department from the Pie Chart grows by 12% next year while all other enrollments remain locked, what will be the new total strength of the college?
12. Suppose the table in Case 1 lists gross expenditure. If the Tax values paid in 2023 and 2024 are retroactively given a flat 15% rebate refund, what is the exact monetary refund received for those two years combined?
13. What is the combined average number of students per branch across just the ME and CE departments?
14. What is the ratio of total fuel expenditure in 2021 and 2022 combined, to the total bonus expenditure in 2024 and 2025 combined?
15. If CS students pay ₹100,000 in tuition and ME students pay ₹50,000, what is the combined total tuition revenue from just these two branches?

### Challenge Questions (2)
16. **Compound Scaling:** (Ref: Vehicle Table). If the production of Sedans scales continuously by a steady 10% year-on-year compound rate starting from the 2025 baseline, calculate the projected sedan manufacturing value for the year 2027.
17. **Cross-Linked Logic:** (Ref: Pie Chart). If 10% of the *total college students* drop out entirely, and every single student who dropped out happened to be from the IT department, what is the new central angle (in degrees) for the IT department on the updated Pie Chart?

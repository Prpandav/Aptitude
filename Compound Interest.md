# Compound Interest - Fundamentals

## 1. Formulas

**Maturity Amount Accumulation:**

$$
\text{Amount (A)} = P \left(1 + \frac{R}{100}\right)^T
$$

**Compound Interest (CI):**

$$
\text{CI} = A - P = P \left[\left(1 + \frac{R}{100}\right)^T - 1\right]
$$

Where:

* **$P$** = **Principal**
* **$R$** = **Rate of interest per annum**
* **$T$** = **Time in years**

**Non-Annual Compounding Refactors:**

If interest is not compounded annually, the formula scales by a cycle factor **$n$**:

* **Half-Yearly (Semi-Annually):** Rate becomes $\frac{R}{2}$, Time becomes $2T$.
* **Quarterly:** Rate becomes $\frac{R}{4}$, Time becomes $4T$.

**2-Year Effective Rate Matrix:**

$$
\text{Effective Rate} = 2R + \frac{R^2}{100}\%
$$

**CI vs SI Discrepancy Formulas:**

* **For 2 Years:**
  $$
  \text{Difference} = P \left(\frac{R}{100}\right)^2
  $$
* **For 3 Years:**
  $$
  \text{Difference} = P \left(\frac{R}{100}\right)^2 \times \left(\frac{300 + R}{100}\right)
  $$

## 2. Key Concepts

**Simple Explanation:** While Simple Interest calculates returns *only* on the original principal, **Compound Interest** calculates interest on the principal **plus any interest accumulated so far**. It is essentially "interest on interest." Every compounding cycle, your earned interest merges into the principal, creating a larger base for the next cycle.

**Real-World Intuition:** Imagine investing **₹10,000** at a **10%** annual rate.

* **Year 1:** You earn 10% of 10,000 = ₹1,000. Your total value becomes ₹11,000.
* **Year 2 (Simple Interest):** You earn another ₹1,000 on the original base $\rightarrow$ ₹12,000.
* **Year 2 (Compound Interest):** You earn 10% on your *new* base of ₹11,000, which is ₹1,100. Your total value becomes ₹12,100.

The extra ₹100 is the interest generated by the first year's interest (10% of ₹1,000). Over long horizons, this effect accelerates dramatically.

- **Exponential Accumulation:** Capital grows non-linearly because previous yield distributions convert into functioning principal asset bases.
- **Compounding Frequency Scaling:** Shorter compounding cycles compress time blocks, meaning internal velocity expansions occur faster at identical nominal annual rates.

## 3. Shortcuts

**Mental Math Trick: Successive Percentage (The Effective Rate)**
For a 2-year horizon, completely skip the formula. Treat the interest rate as a successive percentage change using the formula:

$$
\text{Effective Rate} = 2R + \frac{R^2}{100}\%
$$

*Example:* What is the compound interest on ₹5000 at 6% per annum for 2 years?

* Effective Rate = $2(6) + \frac{36}{100} = 12 + 0.36 = 12.36\%$.
* Find 12.36% of 5000 $\rightarrow 50 \times 12.36 = \text{₹618}$.

**The Constant Delta Matrix (2-Year Baseline)**
The direct mathematical variance between Compound Interest and Simple Interest for a 2-year timeline can be captured without processing separate calculation blocks. Memorize this equation to resolve difference problems in under 10 seconds:

$$
\text{Difference (CI - SI)} = P \left( \frac{R}{100} \right)^2
$$

- **Geometric Compounding Multipliers:** If a sum grows by $K$-times in $Y$ years, it scales to $K^m$-times in exactly $m \times Y$ years.

## 4. Common Mistakes

- **The Chronological Drop Trap:** Assuming the compound interest earned in Year 2 is double the interest earned in Year 1. Year 2 interest includes the premium earned on Year 1's interest payload.
- **The Compounding Frequency Error:** Forgetting to change both $R$ and $T$ simultaneously when shifting from annual to half-yearly or quarterly formats. If you divide $R$ by 2, you *must* multiply $T$ by 2.
- **Applying Linear Calculations:** Applying linear calculation approximations to consecutive balance states instead of executing geometric progression.
- **Shortcut Dangers:** The effective rate approximation formula ($2R + \frac{R^2}{100}$) works flawlessly for a 2-year duration. **Do not use it for 3-year timelines** without executing a third compound leg manually or using the dedicated 3-year discrepancy formula.

## 5. Solved Examples

### Easy

**Problem:** Find the compound interest on **₹10,000** for **2** years at **5%** per annum compounded annually.

* **Step 1:** Calculate the effective 2-year compound rate: $2(5) + \frac{25}{100} = 10 + 0.25 = 10.25\%$.
* **Step 2:** Calculate **10.25%** of the principal (**₹10,000**):

  $$
  \text{CI} = \frac{10.25}{100} \times 10000 = \mathbf{₹1025}
  $$

### Medium

**Problem:** Find the compound interest on **₹8000** for **1** year at **10%** per annum, compounded half-yearly.

* **Step 1:** Adjust variables for half-yearly compounding.

  * New Rate ($R'$) = **5%** per half-year.
  * New Time cycles ($T'$) = $1 \text{ year} \times 2 =$ **2 cycles**.
* **Step 2:** Use the 2-cycle effective rate shortcut: $5 + 5 + \frac{25}{100} = 10.25\%$.
* **Step 3:** Calculate **10.25%** of **8000**:

  $$
  \text{CI} = 8000 \times 0.1025 = 80 \times 10.25 = \mathbf{₹820}
  $$

### Placement-Level

**Problem:** The difference between simple interest and compound interest on a certain sum of money at **10%** per annum for **2** years is **₹65**. Find the principal sum.

* **Step 1:** Determine the total 2-year Simple Interest rate: $10\% \times 2 = 20\%$.
* **Step 2:** Determine the total 2-year Compound Interest rate: $10 + 10 + \frac{100}{100} = 21\%$.
* **Step 3:** Find the percentage difference between CI and SI: $21\% - 20\% = 1\%$.
* **Step 4:** Map this percentage difference to the absolute monetary difference given:

  $$
  1\% \text{ of Principal} = ₹65
  $$

  $$
  \text{Principal} = 65 \times 100 = \mathbf{₹6500}
  $$
